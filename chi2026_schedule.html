What If Moderation Didn't Mean Suppression? A Case for Personalized Content Transformation 🏅 Conflict
AI Personality 🎨 Product Design P1 – Room 121 12 min
Rayhan Rashed, Farnaz Jahanbakhsh · University of Michigan
Click to show abstract
Centralized content moderation paradigm both falls short and overreaches: it fails to account for the subjective nature of harm, and it acts with blunt suppression in response to content deemed harmful, even when such content can be salvaged. The authors investigate this through formative interviews, documenting how seemingly benign content becomes harmful due to individual life experiences. Based on these insights, they developed DIY-MOD, a browser extension that operationalizes a new paradigm: personalized content transformation. Operating on a user's own definition of harm, DIY-MOD transforms sensitive elements within content in real-time instead of suppressing the content itself, selecting the most appropriate transformation from a diverse palette — from obfuscation to artistic stylizing — to match the user's specific needs while preserving the content's informational value.
An Empirical Study to Understand How Students Use ChatGPT for Writing Essays Conflict
Learning, Training, and Self-Development with AI 📖 AI in Education P1 – Room 125 12 min
Andrew Jelson, Daniel Manesh, Alice Jang, Daniel Dunlap, Young-Ho Kim, Sang Won Lee · Virginia Tech, NAVER AI Lab
Click to show abstract
As large language models (LLMs) become widespread, students increasingly turn to systems like ChatGPT for writing tasks. However, how students actually use LLMs for writing remains largely unknown, leaving a limited understanding of their effects on learning. The researchers conducted an online study (n=77) in which students wrote an essay using an in-house ChatGPT, capturing their queries. Through qualitative analysis, they identified the types of assistance students sought, presenting patterns of use ranging from asking for opinions to delegating the entire writing task. They found that students' writing self-efficacy influenced querying patterns, and that levels of ownership and creativity varied depending on usage. This study contributes empirical data to discussions about how writing education should incorporate or regulate LLM-powered tools.

Orca: Browsing at Scale Through User-Driven and AI-Facilitated Orchestration Across Malleable Webpages 🏅
Interactive Prompting, Chaining, and LLM Orchestration Tools 🧠 Human-AI Ctrl P1 – Room 132 12 min
Peiling Jiang, Haijun Xia · UC San Diego
Click to show abstract
Web-based activities span multiple webpages. However, conventional browsers with stacks of tabs cannot support operating and synthesizing large volumes of information across pages. While recent AI systems enable fully automated web browsing and information synthesis, they often diminish user agency and hinder contextual understanding. The authors explore how AI could instead augment user interactions with content across webpages and mitigate cognitive and manual efforts. Through literature on information tasks and web browsing challenges, and an iterative design process, they present novel interactions with their prototype web browser, Orca. Leveraging AI, Orca supports user-driven exploration, operation, organization, and synthesis of web content at scale. To enable browsing at scale, webpages are treated as malleable materials that humans and AI can collaboratively manipulate and compose into a malleable, dynamic, and browser-level workspace. Their evaluation revealed an increased "appetite" for information foraging, enhanced control, and more flexible sensemaking across a broader web information landscape.

Interaction-Augmented Instruction: Modeling the Synergy of Prompts and Interactions in Human-GenAI Collaboration 🏅 Conflict
Interactive Prompting, Chaining, and LLM Orchestration Tools 🧠 Human-AI Ctrl P1 – Room 132 12 min
Leixian Shen, Yifang Wang, Huamin Qu, Xing Xie, Haotian Li · HKUST, Florida State, Microsoft Research Asia
Click to show abstract
Text prompt is the most common way for human-generative AI (GenAI) communication. Though convenient, it is challenging to convey fine-grained and referential intent. One promising solution is to combine text prompts with precise GUI interactions, like brushing and clicking. However, there lacks a formal model to capture synergistic designs between prompts and interactions, hindering their comparison and innovation. To fill this gap, via an iterative and deductive process, the researchers develop the Interaction-Augmented Instruction (IAI) model — a compact entity-relation graph formalizing how the combination of interactions and text prompts enhances human-GenAI communication. With the model, they distill twelve recurring and composable atomic interaction paradigms from prior tools, verifying the model's capability to facilitate systematic design characterization and comparison.

Workshop 📖 AI in Education P1 – Room 122 90 min Workshop website

Click to show abstract
The push to integrate Generative AI (GenAI) into education is framed by its advocates as an inevitable transformation. This workshop invites researchers, educators, and designers to discuss, from a place of self-determination, the critical questions surrounding GenAI in education.
